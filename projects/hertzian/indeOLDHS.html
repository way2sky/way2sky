Hertzian Explorer reveals the invisible electromagnetic waves of computation and communication in our information society. The project relates to the notion of “hertzian space”, a term used by Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby to refer to the invisible physical space created by the electromagnetic radiation radiating from electronic objects.

By bringing to the human scale electronic signals and radiation, the work hopes to help people discover a new perspective on this mysterious but now permanent aspect of our contemporary world, a world immersed in the fleeting and invisible waves of information flow.

Electromagnetic field meters are used to measure the intensity of man-made radiation. This data will be fed in real-time to a custom written Processing program to generate unique visualizations of hertzian space. Different field strengths, their spatial orientation in the XYZ coordinate system, and duration of the radiation will influence the visual result. Celluar automata simulations comprise a second part of the visuals. By reflecting the changing and diverse nature of hertzian space, the CA simulations will explore how environmental factors affect the development of virtual organisms and structures.

Technical and Budgetary Concerns:

An array of single axis AC magnetic field detectors arranged along the X, Y, and Z axis will be used. This allows both measurements of the direction of the field as well as the total field strength of a given area. Retrieving data from these detectors involves basic computer vision technique of brightness tracking the leds on the meters.

Another possible method may involve the use of emf meters with a pc interface feature using serial or USB. The RF meter will handle the detection of radio, microwave, Wi-Fi, and cellular network types of radiation.
